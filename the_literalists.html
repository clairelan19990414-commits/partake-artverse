THE LITERALISTS

A lecture by "Brad" analyzing "literalism"—the willful bad-faith literalization of metaphor—as the common rhetorical engine driving American moral panics, traced across three eras: the 1980s–2000s Satanic Panic, the 2010s campus call-out crusades, and the current "cavefab" era of performative outrage without genuine ideological conviction.

⏱ ~0 min ✦ Brad Troemel 📍 Patreon
Key Concepts
Literalism
The willful misinterpretation of metaphors by taking them as literally as possible, ignoring context and intent, to reframe debate by casting oneself as righteous and opponents as purely evil.
Central analytical lens unifying all three moral panic eras in the talk
Apophilia
Identifying a pattern because you want it to be there; starting from a conclusion and cherry-picking distorted evidence to support a desired outcome.
Cognitive mechanism underlying literalist thinking
Moral panic
Recurring societal fear campaigns that target cultural scapegoats, sustained by institutional authority buy-in from media, police, and religious leaders.
Recurring historical phenomenon the speaker analyzes through the lens of literalism
Literalist instrumentalism of art
The belief that art's sole legitimate purpose is to reflect consensus morality as directly and unambiguously as possible; any ambiguity is treated as suspicious or dangerous.
Explains why literalists produce dull, predetermined culture and critique complex art
Literalist impressionability thesis
The belief that audiences passively and totally absorb the moral character of whatever culture they consume, as though words and images are spells cast on blank minds.
Justifies moral panic campaigns by framing audiences (especially children) as helpless victims of culture
Campus Crusades
2010s liberal college campus moral panic treating language as a form of literal violence, elevating the stakes of speech and driving cancellations and mandatory public apologies.
Second major case study, showing how literalism migrated from Christian conservatism to campus liberalism
Social cleansing / cancellation
Public shunning of an individual as a ritual surrogate sacrifice meant to symbolically destroy a larger social evil (e.g., canceling one curator to 'kill' racism itself).
Key mechanism of campus-era literalism; explains disproportionate punishment
Trigger warnings
Content advisories preceding potentially upsetting material, serving primarily as an a priori ideological alliance signal rather than genuine medical protection.
Symptom of literalist culture in the 2010s; functions as a loyalty oath to the consensus
Cavefab culture
Post-2020 era in which literalist performances—calling opponents fascists, satanists, groomers—continue as purely symbolic communal rituals after genuine ideological conviction has eroded.
Third case study; explains how literalism survives the collapse of true belief
Provocation cycle
An engineered outrage loop in which an influencer creates content designed to bait a predictable literalist reaction from opponents, which then rallies their own audience through shared enemy hatred.
Shows how literalism is now commercially weaponized; renders consensus as brave vanguard
Microaggression
Minor linguistic choices construed as subtle forms of literal violence, elevating the stakes of everyday speech for college-educated professionals.
Core concept in campus crusades era; extended the logic of violence to all communicative conflict
Literalist cultural production
Art made to serve a predetermined ideological message directly, with form as an afterthought; the speaker argues its lack of ambiguity is its selling point for its audience.
Outcome of literalism applied to creation rather than criticism; seen in both Christian and liberal media

Movements & Periods
1980s–2000s
Satanic Panic
Widespread American moral panic asserting that Satanism was infiltrating society through daycare centers and mainstream culture; fueled false criminal prosecutions of daycare workers and cultural boycotts of games, music, and films
2010s
Campus Call-Out Culture (Campus Crusades)
Liberal college campus movement that expanded the definition of violence to include language, driving public cancellations, mandatory apologies, trigger warnings, and a culture of constant self-censorship among the college-educated
2020s–present
Cavefab Culture
Post-conviction era in which culture-war literalist performances (labeling opponents fascists, satanists, groomers) continue as hollow communal rituals and belonging signals after genuine ideological belief has collapsed
1970s–1990s
Televangelism
Christian broadcast movement that reached peak influence during the Satanic Panic, using it to generate renewed evangelical purpose and audience as church attendance began its long decline
1980s–1990s
Child Safety Movement
Broader cultural push to protect children from danger, coinciding with the Satanic Panic; produced zero-tolerance policies, playground safety regulations, warning labels, and the 'latchkey kid' anxiety driving parental guilt
1960s–1980s
Second Wave Feminism
Feminist movement cited as enabling upper-middle-class women to choose careers over full-time homemaking, indirectly producing the 'latchkey kid' phenomenon and parental anxiety that fueled Satanic Panic scapegoating of daycare centers
2015–present
Cancel Culture
Social media-driven campaigns of public shaming over perceived language violations; framed by the speaker as digital moral panics functioning as pressure-valve rituals for suppressed social anxiety
2017–present
QAnon
Far-right conspiracy theory (speaker references 'saving the mole children') cited as a cavefab example—believers continue performing the ritual even after knowing it was fraudulent
2013–present
Black Lives Matter
Racial justice movement cited as a cavefab example—white allies maintain BLM links in bios as belonging signals even after the speaker claims the movement's organizational credibility collapsed
1990s–present
Christian Alternative Media
Literalist Christian cultural production (VeggieTales, Christian rock, straight-to-VHS films) created as safe, unambiguous alternatives to mainstream culture; speaker argues it exemplifies how literalism produces inferior art by subordinating form to message
