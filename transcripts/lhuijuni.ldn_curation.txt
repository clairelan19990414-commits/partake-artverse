    Core thesis:
    This creator operates at the intersection of personal finance and late-stage capitalism critique, examining how economic systems shape bodies, lifestyles, and aspirations—particularly for high-earning women in expensive cities like London. She reframes wealth not as a number but as a quality-of-life question, using frameworks like time affluence and the HENRY trap to expose the gap between earning well and feeling rich. Her lens is observational and slightly sardonic: capitalism produces absurdities (£300 gyms, body-as-brand culture, financial nihilism) and she documents them as a fellow participant, not a detached critic.

    Key ideas:
    - Time affluence as the real metric of wealth (income beyond a threshold buys time, not happiness)
- Body-as-brand / body-as-status-symbol — self-optimization reframed as capitalist signaling
- The HENRY trap — structural ceiling where high earners remain asset-poor in high-cost cities
- Financial nihilism among Gen Z — homeownership and wealth-building feel structurally impossible
- Niche financial actors as narrative hooks — Mrs Watanabe FX traders, short sellers as market protagonists
- Geopolitical economics as personal finance context — Chinese soft power, Trump put, macro forces on everyday money
- Late-stage capitalism as shared absurdity — luxury wellness costs, overpriced lifestyle goods critiqued from inside the system
