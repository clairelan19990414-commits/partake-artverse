    Core thesis:
    David Kyle Choe profiles founder-led brands — especially underappreciated non-Western ones (Chinese, Japanese, Korean) — to extract the principled thinking behind their longevity and cultural resonance. His central argument is that enduring brands are built on deep conviction and a strong point of view, not scale or conventional marketing tactics. He is increasingly interested in how AI will force a rethinking of what brand identity even means.

    Key ideas:
    - Founder principles as durable brand DNA — conviction and original intent outlast acquisitions, new leadership, and cultural shifts (Arc'teryx, Din Tai Fung, Chrome Hearts)
- Non-Western brands (Chinese, Japanese, Korean) quietly outcompeting the West in categories the West thought it owned (To Summer, Laopu, Onitsuka Tiger, Luckin, HeyTea)
- Anti-scale / intentional scarcity as a brand moat — refusing ecommerce, ads, or conventional distribution to protect brand integrity (Chrome Hearts, Aman)
- Strong POV as the single most important variable in brand building — differentiation flows from having something real to say, not from marketing execution
- Dynastic and founder-led stewardship — family or founder continuity preserves brand soul in ways professional management rarely replicates (Din Tai Fung, Snow Peak)
- AI as a branding inflection point — when bots mediate discovery, brands must rethink how positioning and differentiation are communicated beyond human feeling
- Original perspective as a scarce, appreciating asset — as AI floods the information supply, independent thought and curation become the highest-value signal
- Worldbuilding depth as competitive advantage — the willingness to go further than anyone else in craft, story, or creative volume is what separates generational brands
