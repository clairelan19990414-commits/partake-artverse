@etymologynerd notes

    Thesis:
    @etymologynerd creates infographic-based content that traces the word origins of everyday objects, places, and concepts — showing how language acts as a compressed archive of human history, culture, and migration. The creator's thesis is that etymology is universally accessible and endlessly surprising: virtually any domain (food, geography, science, music) reveals hidden linguistic patterns once you look. Most recently, this framework has been extended into a book arguing that social media algorithms are the newest force reshaping how language evolves.

    Key concepts:
    - Etymology as hidden history: Words function as compressed records of cultural exchange, human migration, and historical discovery, making linguistics a form of archaeology. (via CAnIRdiA5Ow, CaCjk4ZuriT, B99HGTKAZA7)
- Toponym patterns: Place names encode layers of colonial, indigenous, and immigrant history, making geography a readable map of power and settlement. (via CaCjk4ZuriT, CAnIRdiA5Ow)
- Scientific naming conventions: Taxonomy, anatomy, medicine, and geology follow systematic Latin and Greek construction rules that preserve the logic of early scientific thought. (via CcaawemOJ1p, B99HGTKAZA7)
- Algospeak: Platform content moderation and recommendation algorithms now actively drive vocabulary formation, producing terms like 'unalive' as users adapt language to evade filters. (via DDHqAcvOF0x)
- Brand and cultural naming: Artist names, art movement labels, typeface names, and currency terms reflect the values, aesthetics, and power structures of the era that coined them. (via CPx3luegPNt, CRgpE72ADEi, Ctob1J5g7l3)
- Cross-domain universality: The same etymological mechanisms — metaphor, borrowing, eponymy — operate identically across unrelated fields, suggesting language change follows deep structural patterns. (via CPx3luegPNt, CcaawemOJ1p, Ctob1J5g7l3, CRgpE72ADEi)
- Visual/spatial etymology mapping: Presenting word origins as infographics and geographic maps makes abstract linguistic history legible and shareable for general audiences. (via CAnIRdiA5Ow, CaCjk4ZuriT, CPx3luegPNt)

    Positions:
    - Algorithms are now an active linguistic force: platform moderation produces vocabulary ('unalive', 'skibidi', 'cottagecore') the same way trade routes and conquest once did.
- Etymology is universally accessible — any domain reveals hidden linguistic patterns once you apply the same lens.
- Words carry compressed cultural records: to know a word's origin is to recover a piece of history that written records may have lost.
- Visual presentation (infographics, maps) is not just a delivery format but an argument — it demonstrates that etymology has spatial and systemic logic, not just trivia value.
