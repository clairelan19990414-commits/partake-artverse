Chapter five, education, migration, gentrification. It would be naive to think hipsters moved to places like Williamsburg or Bushwick solely because of all the cool articles they read online. Hipsters are part of the broader millennial generation, which shared three distinct feats. Millennials are the most educated, indebted, and likely to have tried and failed at living away from home. These three attributes paint a picture of the hipster gentrifier as a uniquely cursed figure. From the 90s on, Millennials were encouraged to attend college with an intensity like never before as it became apparent that the job market was shifting toward service economy professions that required education beyond high school. All the historical data up to that point showed that getting a college degree automatically led to higher earning potential and job security. The problem with this way of thinking is that it didn't account for the drastically different returns on investment that different college degrees provided for their soaring tuition costs. So while the majority of Millennials with a STEM degree paid back their student loans in under five years, we now know that most of those who received degrees in the more hipster inclined fields of visual arts, music, and humanities will never be able to repay their loans. In a stark reversal, the hipsters who received these negative ROI degrees have, on average, a lower net income and savings, the Millennials who only got their high school diploma. When you look at their unemployment rate and wages, you come to realize the astonishing fact that many hipsters effectively educated themselves out of the middle class. While you can get a creative degree anywhere, there's just a handful of cities in America with a real job market for cultural creativity, and each of them is mercilessly competitive. The more creative degrees you have, the more likely you are to live away from home, and the more debt you're likely to have, meaning the people at the colonial frontier of gentrification tended to be those who didn't have many other options for where they could afford to live post-graduation. Although hipsters moving into low income neighborhoods frequently worked, low income jobs, many believed these hipsters college education would over time afford them the kind of upward mobility that was unavailable to locals. Looking back, we can now see just how little upward mobility all those creative degrees afforded hipsters. In the media, there was a prevailing belief that hipsters were callously indifferent to the displacement their post college migration caused. However, hipsters were conditioned to understand themselves as an evil force displacing locals. The same liberal college debt that incentivized hipsters to move to low income neighborhoods was the same college experience that granted them the enlightened perspective of racialized self-loathing for being there. It's important to understand what is self-hating and horizontally critical about hipsters because this behavior was later adopted by their mid-2010's cultural successor. The activist, after hipster culture faded into being universal, the activist became the new way for young people to socially distinguish themselves from their conservative, suburban coded, non-college-educated peers. Both cultures operated around the anxiety of exposing yourself as being late to the scene. The activist and the hipster could only alleviate this fear by doing ever more diligent internet research and performing public displays of refined self-awareness. The two have many parallels because most millennials transformed into activists once their tenure as hipsters ended. There are, however, some significant differences between the two. The activists took many of the political causes which operated as subtext for the hipster and made them their pretext. Avert displays of political engagement became the way activist competed with each other, calling one another liberal as a pejorative to separate the authentic from the inauthentic, just as the word hipster previously functioned. Markers of distinction, like the music you listen to, fell out of favor as an indirect way of identifying yourself as a culturally refined college graduate. And its place came a literal emphasis on the subjects taught in a liberal arts education. Digital discourse, rather than cultural consumption, became the new aesthetic battleground where the war over coolness was waged. Activists now use this coolness to find jobs, or otherwise compensate for their low pay at the job they have, by providing them a sense of status. The difference is that while the hipster acknowledged what was superficial about the fleeting fashion ability of coolness by adopting an ironic posture towards it, the activists is entirely self-serious in their engagement with a fleeting set of topics that are subject to the same ebbs and flows of fashion ability. Though their seasonal obsession with new profile pictures and adjutant prop abides by all the same socially reinforced conventions of coolness, the activist isn't capable of acknowledging coolness as their motive, because it makes their political convictions appear self-motivated rather than self-less. For the activist, all the traditional hallmarks of youth culture are still there, but rendered as digital discourse instead of social experience. The desire for sex is sublimated as digital discussions of sexual identity in the consumption of porn through digitized sex work. The desire for drugs is sublimated as digital discussions of therapy and doctor prescribed pharmaceuticals. The desire for fashion is sublimated into a fast-paced exchange of digital images and mystified phrases perpetually subject to change. For the hipster, there was the corrupting feeling that coolness was at stake in every endeavor, that nothing could be pursued for its own sake. For the activist, there's a similar pressure at understand everything you do through the lens of its political instrumentality. The only justifiable reason for you to express yourself, whether through a status update, a t-shirt you're selling or a party you're throwing, is to do so in direct service of a political cause, either through awareness raising or fundraising. Variations on the phrase people are dying became the solemn reminder that nothing could be created much less enjoyed without demonstrating an overt political commitment as the reason for doing so.
