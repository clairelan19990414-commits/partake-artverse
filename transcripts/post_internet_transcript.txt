In Art Thoughts, Youngman doles out criticisms of race and class in the art world by identifying power dynamics as though they were advised to be followed. His gift was in taking art world concepts which were complicated by art speak and simplifying them down to their core. The comedy of his videos came from the fact that his core descriptions were always accurate, showing just how much unnecessary pretentious jargon was being used to distract us from the truth about art. Basically relational aesthetics is when someone with an MFA wants to meet new people but because they spent all that time pursuing an MFA they don't know how to talk to people normally and they got really poor social skills and they can't find no other way to meet new people other than forcing them into odd activities at their own poorly attended art opening. Another popular aggregator of art world concepts from that time was work. Years before Who's Who appeared on Instagram work pioneered the practice of posting multiple artists work who shared a common theme. Artists were sometimes sensitive to this because they believed any similarity identified between them and someone else was an assault on their originality but I think that's the wrong read on the project. Work created unexpected links between artists across generations showing how all of art was part of a vast network of influence. At a time when the locationless post internet scene felt so distant from the rest of the art world it was refreshing to see how we were actually all connected. Calling post internet a scene without a territory might not be entirely accurate because there were plenty of in-person exhibition spaces it's just that they weren't located in Manhattan. Ivan wrote an awful essay about them called the emergence of dual sites in 2010. The essay covered galleries like Extra Extra, Appendix, Future Gallery, Gambia Castle, Jericho Ditch, New Jersey, Paloma Presents, Preteen Gallery, and Reference Art Gallery. All these dual sites shared two things a location somewhere outside the art world's power centers and a programming schedule which reflected the type of post internet work being shared online. The term dual site referred to their double objective these were physical spaces whose purpose largely hinged on producing digital installation images for the internet. This was where their true traffic would happen as thousands of people logged on to see who was featured in their latest show. I look back at the burgeoning network of homespun galleries and blog coverage of the late 2000s post internet scene as a natural result of what happens when you bring a critical mass of artists together and they have to figure out what they collectively value. When enough people start living next to each other then you need to create laws and roads and currency and all the things required of a functioning society. The fact that there is no institutional or commercial support meant these inter-scene relations needed to be self-governed and the entity which most closely resembled a government for post internet art was this magazine. I don't think this was the role Lauren, Marco, David, Solomon, Nick, and Patrick aimed for but it was the benevolent community organizer function that everyone trusted them to perform. They were probably the only group no other group had beef with which is what made them such a trustworthy mint for the only currency the post internet scene had at that point which was clout. This is clout UBI program meant that if you were an artist, DJ, filmmaker, fashion designer, writer, or model then you too could have your work featured on their site or in one of their exhibitions or parties. They really had so many different outlets to provide other people visibility that they provided much of the scene's infrastructure. Their New York centric curation was inclusive enough to forge unexpected links between strangers but it was still exclusive enough to maintain a standard of quality which made people want to be involved. In their own art practice, dis-pioneered the creation of speculative spawncon using the fashion magazine editorial format as their medium to propose absurdist trends and brand collaborations though it was never clear if they had the brand's blessing to do so. Their editorial images defied categorization. They were too fashionable to be purely satirical and too artistic to be purely commercial. This is speculative trends were further developed when they founded their own stock photo agency which in typical dis-fashion they offered up as an opportunity for dozens of other artists to participate in. Where normal stock photo agencies aimed to be as universal as possible dis-images functioned by staging impossibly niche images for the present moment and speculative fantasies for the not so distant future. The resulting stock photos feel as though they were made by an algorithm using every popular keyword at once creating images which become alien through their familiarity. This process of exhausting every mimetic combination possible was more or less the goal of jogging, a blog I helped manage from 2009 to 2014. Jogging used a tumbler submission system where 1200 people contributed over 5,000 posts conversationally making art in response to one another. Together jogging contributors created a series of in-house memes which could roughly be divided into three categories. Photoshop to seat, destroyed technology, and food sculptures. Photoshop to seat was the cornerstone of jogging posts because that's what the blog started out as. A series of installation images of sculptures I digitally edited to include parts I stole from Google image search because I couldn't afford extra materials. These digitally altered images were posted alongside unedited sculptures which were all given a uniform titling scheme. Goading the viewer into asking whether the post was real or not was jogging's first viral strategy. By the end of jogging, this Photoshop to seat reached its pinnacle in the work of Edward Marshall Schenke who created a series of conspiracy theory posts that collapsed into themselves, identifying patterns within patterns until the process short circuited. These images feel like they're approaching a recognizable conspiracy theory by making use of authentic tea party graphic design. Subject matter sourced from Facebook only to be revealed as a schizo mythology that refers to nothing more than itself rather than explaining the secrets of the outside world. Destroyed technology posts which were originated by Aaron Noah Graham, Sean C. Smith and Will Shea were some of jogging's other most viral images. These posts took off because they had the weird effect of triggering people in two completely opposite ways. On one hand destroyed technology pissed people off because of how wasteful and privileged the gesture was. While on the other hand people would happily express a sense of catharsis from viewing these images like watching the weight of their addiction to their devices finally be lifted. Destroying technology has since become a standard tactic used to go viral across social media. Then there were the food sculpture posts on jogging which were popular because everybody had access to a banana or egg in their kitchen so it provided a thrifty set of options every contributor could use to respond to each other no matter where they were. If you saw someone post a sandwich sculpture in the morning you could reply to them with your own sandwich sculpture by lunchtime. In 2012 I created an Etsy store to offer my own food sculptures for sale using the money to pay jogging contributors. The products in the store played on the dissonance between the idealized viral image being advertised and the disgusting envelope of rotting taco meat the buyer received in the mail. I like the idea of literalizing the new dynamic I saw between art objects and images where the art object had become subservient to the digital images ability to be dispersed as widely as possible. These types of ideas were most directly articulated in Artie Virkant's 2010 essay The Image Object Post Internet which described a view of art where there is no longer a real or singular version of an artwork as a physical object alone. That what you see in a gallery exists alongside images of it online and in magazines alongside images people take of it and post to their own social media accounts alongside any meme or recontextualized version of the art. That together all these things form a constellation of an artwork's existence which is constantly subject to change. The Image Object Post Internet describes how the installation image always becomes the central focus of this new constellation because it's the primary entry point people have into knowing the work exists. Virkant's own art was part of a group of post-internet photographers who made image alteration the method and subject of their work. Artists like Kate Statue, Josh Sidorella, Harm Vanden Dorpel, and Lucas Blalock used real images to render them fake or faked images made to look real by creating authentic looking installation images of galleries which never existed. All of these photoshoped tricksters including myself were deeply inspired by Oliver Larek's 2009 video essay Versions which described how photoshopped images of news events, celebrities, and history create a digital media landscape where authenticity is decided on by the viewer. The more an image is viewed the more likely it is to wind up at the top of search results creating a dynamic where truth becomes determined by viewers in real time. Larek describes the older compulsion to have just a single universally recognized image as the truth as being a remnant from monotheistic religion's rules against idolatry where no one was allowed to create copies of images of God. Larek says the increase of different versions of an image doesn't diminish aura but increases it by turning the image into a living organism able to update itself in ways a single official version can. Every altered image creates a parallel world, the world in which it's true for that audience. Years later in 2013 I wrote an essay called The Accidental Audience which mapped out what happens when art goes viral and is shared by online audiences who have no idea what they're viewing was once considered art or made by an artist. This way of courting multiple potentially hostile audience interpretations was as influenced by Andy Kaufman as it was by social media where bitter flame wars arguing over a post meaning guarantee maximum visibility. And just like pro wrestlers the best image anarchists performed as heel or babyface characters to give their audiences a moral stake in what they were viewing. The most famous of these performances was Sam Hyde's paradigm shift 2070 from 2013 because we're just gonna kill him. Hyde managed to get on stage by convincing the organizers of Ted X Drexel that he was a photojournalist documenting the woman trash pickers of Somalia. The Ted talk that ensued satirized everything from clickbait cultural liberalism to Silicon Valley Techno Utopiaism to the airport bookstore intellectualism. Dressed as a Roman soldier, Hyde's Ted talk leaps from one self congratulatory platitude to the next, offering a series of patently insane solutions to fix all the world's problems by using as many buzzwords as humanly possible. What inspires me is teaching African refugees how to program JavaScript. What inspires me is finding out how to use maglev trains to get resources to the moon. These are the challenges that tomorrow's gonna face. Okay? How are we gonna get clean drinking water to 2 billion Chinese people? You got an answer? Get inspired. Throughout the video you can see the nervous and confused expressions of the Ted talk's onlookers as the camera occasionally pans in their direction. These people are less an audience than an extension of Hyde's performance. Pollution! War! These are big problems! Fix it! Now! Paradigm Shift 2070 was ultimately intended for YouTube, making the entire in-person performance just a staging ground for its digital release. By contrast, Amalia Ollman's 2014 performance, Excellences and Perfections, debuted directly to Instagram. Without telling anyone she was performing, Ollman documented the tumultuous path of her fictional life. As she moved to the big city, broke up with her long-term boyfriend, did drugs, had plastic surgery, self-destructed, apologized, recovered, and found a new boyfriend. Throughout these life events, Ollman shifted between different feminine Instagram aesthetics, going from a pastel artsy soft girl to a cocaine-fueled bougie thought to a lean-in yoga feminist. Excellences and Perfections is a performance which was only complete once Ollman revealed herself to be performing, giving viewers the space to reflect on just how drastically their perception of her had changed. The takeaway being that everyone is following a script of their performed identity online. The only difference between a truly authentic personal brand and a total hoax is the latter's willingness to admit what they're doing is fake. Another one of my favorite performances of Transformation online came from Conor O'Malley in 2013 on Vine. Over the course of a year and a half, O'Malley's six-second videos tell the story of a man's descent into madness as his daily bike path to work through lower Manhattan forces him to come into contact with some of the richest people alive. The videos began as a series of goofy street interactions. Quick comments, questions, requests for fist bumps from unsuspecting suits. He gives the impression of being someone who really looks up to these traders, a kind of hustler to be, but as the videos go on, O'Malley's character gains a perverse class consciousness about his own status as a lowly cyclist compared to the luxury car-driving Wall Street professionals he can never become. Rather than growing resentful, O'Malley starts aggressively worshiping the stock traders, praising them to their faces as financial gods given the mandate of heaven over him. The videos grow more unhinged as O'Malley's character fully descends into madness, no longer capable of full sentences, only monosyllabic screams shouted as loud as possible towards the financial gods of his worship. In time, he grows unable to distinguish between wealthy people and the fine restaurants, sports cars, and yachts which define their lives, shouting praise at inanimate luxury objects in the middle of the night. In the end, O'Malley's character dies alone on the streets of Manhattan. Looking back at all the post-internet artists who sought out their peers intentionally and audiences accidentally, it makes me wonder how much they were motivated by the fact that the commercial art world didn't care about what they were doing. Would they have created such an enormous scene without a territory if they were given a warm welcome into the art world? Chapter 3 – The Commercial Turn All these questions would soon answer themselves by the mid-2010s, as commercial arts economy suddenly became dependent on iPhones and social media. This digital adoption among art world professionals led to a curiosity about all the artists who'd already been making work on social media for the past decade, creating a new era of commercial opportunities for post-internet artists. No one explained the sudden post-internetification of arts economy better than Michael Sanchez in his essay Art and Transmission, where he identified 2011 as the year when the iPhone dramatically expanded the commercial reach of the art world by providing dealers and collectors with the ability to view high-resolution images instantaneously. Before then, galleries were scheduled seasonally. Museums scheduled yearly. Print magazines scheduled monthly. The whole thing was fairly slow. But when the older art world professionals finally went digital, it had the same effect on them that it had on post-internet artists by speeding up their entire industry's timeline. Exhibitions could now be viewed before they even opened. So you no longer had to wait any time at all to forward the art for sale to whatever collector, advisor, or curator who might be interested. Ultimately, the reason the previously internet-averse art world became digital was purely a matter of survival. iPhones and social media provided dealers such a major advantage over their competitors who waited a week for installation images to come back or a month for the print review to come out. Universal adoption happened when galleries realized they needed to become post-internet, or else their competitors would beat them to the sale. As Sanchez describes, it might seem like this would also speed up the process of building a critical consensus around art. But the speed actually wound up disabling judgment in favor of collective attention. In other words, critical consensus became determined not by what everyone thought was good, but by what everyone was looking at, commenting on, sharing with each other. Getting a positive review in a magazine was nice, but ultimately useless, whereas having a mega-collector post a selfie with your work became priceless. iPhones and social media made whatever work was most visible the most collectively valued by default. In becoming commercially beholden to social media, the art world adopted its worldview that there's no such thing as being qualitatively good or bad because no one has time to articulate all that in under 150 characters. There are only artworks which get attention or get ignored, things that sell or things that don't. Line goes up or line goes down. There's no better example of this maximum practice than contemporary art daily, which replaced art forum as the most influential art media outlet without so much as writing a single word about the exhibitions they endorsed. A good review on CAD meant your exhibition was posted on CAD, and a bad review on CAD meant your exhibition was not posted on CAD. The website's popularity was built on redirecting traffic away from individual gallery websites and magazines to become the primary point of access for information about new shows. CAD's web design approximated being in a gallery by offering a clean white layout with barely any text breaking up its scrollable wall of images. In turn, galleries looked to optimize themselves to be posted on CAD by ditching their traditional spotlighting in favor of high wattage fluorescence, which gave their interiors a uniformly lit, almost 3D modeled appearance. Much has been made about how these catified viewing conditions specifically led to zombie formalism, a type of bland, abstract painting popular in the mid-2010s that featured a uniform drizzling of texture all over its surface as a collector-friendly throwback to modernist movements like abstract expressionism. But I think there's a much more simple answer as to what this newly standardized digital viewing format led to, which was a whole lot more paintings in general. An installation image of a painting shows the entire work at once. You don't need multiple views of it like you would a sculpture. When viewed in high resolution on a laptop, paintings offered a pretty faithful depiction of what collectors could expect from their purchase. It's definitely ironic that the medium which commercially benefited the most from all this newfangled technology was the oldest art medium in existence. Contemporary art daily's strategy of getting upstream of galleries' websites to become the primary point of contact was mirrored in a new breed of art speculators who were emerging at the same time. These dealer-collector hybrids would become known as Flippers, a name which referred to their four-step sales strategy, which consisted of Step 1, scouting social media for emerging artists. Step 2, DMing those artists to purchase a bulk quantity of their paintings for a discounted price. Step 3, promoting and selling some of those paintings to other collectors to create speculative interest in the artist's work. And Step 4, once the feeding frenzy's been initiated, flipping the remaining paintings they own at auction for an exponentially larger return. In hindsight, there's probably no group of artists more perfect for what Flippers were looking for than the post-internet scene at that time. Post-internet artists' websites provided links to their entire social network, creating a very streamlined scouting process. Post-internet artists were cash-poor and clout-rich, making them eager to sell their work at a discount and highly capable of helping generate speculative buzz on social media. Post-internet artists had a legitimate claim to avant-garde newness by making work about a digital subject the entire art world was suddenly adopting. The two fit together perfectly. Compounding this speculative buzz was Hans Ulrich Obrus' 2013 curatorial project 89+, which was basically a cattle call for every post-internet artist in existence, giving some 300 artists the old H.U.O. brand of institutional approval. Though it promised to be some kind of richer link-later project by revisiting these artists every couple years for the rest of their lives, the project was much more short-lived and transactional. Just like CAD or the Flippers, 89+, was Obrus' attempt to get upstream of museums by laying the absurd claim to having personally discovered an entire generation of artists all at once. With this added institutional push, soon there were all types of Flippers eagerly hopping in post-internet artists' DMs, offering $5,000 in exchange for 100 paintings. For Flippers, these offers to buy in bulk were strategic. They needed to have an enormous inventory to be able to sell to as many other collectors as possible, while still having enough left over so they could truly profit at auction later on. This is why Flipping catered to artists who made a high volume of similar-looking, small-scale, easily shippable work, hence the trading card frenzy of 2014. Just as post-internet artists were incentivized by social media to never stop posting, they were now incentivized by a social media-driven market to never stop painting. Like the conceptual artists of the 1960s were made to aesthetically compromise with their corporate sponsors, so too were the post-internet artists forced to aesthetically compromise with their flipper patrons. I don't mean to cast too much gloom on this moment because the reality was everyone I knew who was making a living from art for the first time in their life was ecstatic, even if the money was fairly modest in hindsight. People got studios, they didn't have to work four different part-time jobs, they could go out to eat at a restaurant. After years of post-2008 gloom, it was a fun moment. But I think a lot of artists of that generation, myself included, didn't fully understand the trade-off we were making. Making art went from a process of communication with your peers to a process of translation for your collectors. Unfortunately, what was lost in translation were many of the things that made post-internet art distinct. When you go through all the work I previously mentioned, there's a lot of traits those projects share in common. Things like digital interactivity or going viral and reaching accidental audiences or telling a story over long stretches of time from post to post or conversationally making work and dialogue with your peers or responding to the world around you in real time. All these things that post-internet art did so well weren't possible in the same way through discrete paintings. Ultimately, I don't resent the commercial turd because we all sold out or something like that. I resent it because it led a lot of great artists down a garden path that eventually made them quit making art altogether. Let me use a little personal experience here to explain what I mean. So let's say you just cash your first check from translating your previous post-internet work into new paintings and you're thinking to yourself, wow, okay, this is a natural progression. This is how every artist matures. So you do a few more solo shows and this works for a little while. But once you're in that world long enough, what you realize is that the reason people first appreciated your paintings was because they translated this other authentically digital project from before. But now you've geared your studio around making gallery-ready paintings and you can't afford to go back to make digital things for free again. You need to keep churning out your trading cards. This means the way your new work evolves becomes more insular. It becomes self-referential to the formal language of your last round of gallery work, growing increasingly distant from the post-internet work which first gave it authenticity until there's none left. At which point, you're just making formal art. You're no longer a post-internet artist translating what was a conceptual project into paintings. Now you've just become a formal painter left to compete with other painters who make work which is probably more sellable than yours because it's the exclusive skill set they've been developing their entire adult life. This means that when your work stops selling, you become lost because the artistic compromise you made for financial security no longer provides financial security. Now you're just compromised. And this was the position so many post-internet artists found themselves in five years out from their first sales push during the commercial turn. It was the position I was in when I started a Patreon which is why I understand why so many post-internet artists quit making art because for as tough as it is to be a starving artist, it's even tougher to go from being a starving artist to a decently fed artist to a starving artist all over again. I don't want to give the impression that post-internet art stopped in the mid-2010s, quite the opposite. We still live in a post-internet paradigm. The subtle triumph of post-internet art is that its strategies and themes have become so widely adopted it's now more or less synonymous with contemporary art. Where a decade ago people might have pointed and said oh that looks post-internet today they just say that's just what art looks like. But if you turn the lens in reverse to look back at the past half century of work made at the intersection of art and technology you can see two distinct sides of a spectrum come into view. On one end there's work made by techno-utopians and obsessives the type that places a blind faith in technology as the key to artistic progress to the extent that it values technology more than art itself. It's art made in obedience to the incentives created by technology aesthetic or otherwise. On the other end of the spectrum is the work which accepts technology's influence as a fact of life making use of technology for its utility as a tool like any other. This art doesn't see technology as a portal into a utopian future or a get rich quick scheme but as a condition of our existence whose massive influence on our lives warrants an equally critical response. No work made at the intersection of art and technology ever exists purely on one side of the spectrum or the other because our subjectivity means art can never be purely technological and any art made using technology will always be a compromise with engineers.
