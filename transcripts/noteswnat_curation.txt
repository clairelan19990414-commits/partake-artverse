    Core thesis:
    @noteswnat argues that deliberate, cognitively demanding activities — reading classic literature, learning instruments, dancing, doing things the hard way — build measurable brain resilience and protect against cognitive decline, backed by research citations. The creator positions analog hobbies and challenging consumption (classics over self-help, books over scrolling) as superior to passive entertainment for both intelligence and social attractiveness. The underlying framework is that the brain must be continuously challenged across multiple domains simultaneously to build "cognitive reserve" — its defense system against aging and mediocrity.

    Key ideas:
    - Cognitive reserve — building neural backup pathways through multi-domain hobbies to slow aging and defer dementia
- Friction-maxxing — deliberately choosing harder methods (handwriting, in-person, manual) to strengthen brain over convenience
- Classics over self-help — literary fiction builds independent critical thinking; prescriptive content tells you what to think
- Four-quadrant hobby taxonomy — physical, learning, memory, and creative hobbies each protect a different cognitive system
- AI-proof skill premium — emotional intelligence, messy decision-making, and genuine creativity increase in value as AI handles technical tasks
- Social hobbies as neuroprotection — loneliness literally shrinks gray matter; group activities reverse the damage
- Optimal difficulty principle — the brain only builds new pathways when challenged; easy or repetitive tasks offer no cognitive upside
- Metacognition as trainable skill — thinking about how you think, tracking reasoning errors, and teaching others are learnable habits
