That absence of legacy fuels the third hipster strategy for surviving slop world, building a prestigious canon of culture that exists beyond the trough. If all slop, whether self-aware or not, is disposable, then the only way out is through culture that aims to endure. Prestige TV, a tour cinema, risky solo shows, the hope is that somewhere, someone is still making art that matters. Prestige promises meaning in a culture built for disposability. What seems like the contradiction of our time, the idea that everyone has infinite instantaneous access to whatever content they want, and yet no one cares to watch any of it, starts to make more sense when you zoom in on the word content, which implies that everything now available is cheap, generic, and disposable. Prestige, by contrast, is expensive, distinctive, and lasting. This craving isn't new. Canon building has long been central to hipsterdom, which always walked a line between gatekeeping snobbery and the sincere urge to evangelize the overlook, which changed as the urgency in the face of inshidification and the loss of faith in cultural institutions, prestige building feels less like a hobby and more like an emergency rescue mission to salvage meaning from the wreckage. Online, much of prestige building is done through nostalgic historicization. Every hipster corner of the internet has its own main character, biblical text, forbidden film, cancel genius, misunderstood flop, foundational meme, and golden era. These fragments get reassembled into a kind of secular canon, not enforced from above, but constantly gestured at through references, playlists, screenshots, fancams, think pieces, and video essays. It's not just accounts with Greek statues as their PFPs. Every digital subculture is attempting to return to its own prestigious past. Newly created culture, on the other hand, is put through a gauntlet, whenever a project threatens to enter the canon of prestige, hipster corners of social media quickly turn into art media seminar classes, where praise, overanalysis, and preemptive backlash all compete. Together, their debates become a grassroots prestige filter. Around release dates, the discourse becomes so frenzy that participants even avoid social media for fear of spoilers, arguably the only force strong enough to get the chronically online to log off. But this desperation also warps the discourse. Evangelists become self-indulgent stands, elevating middling projects. At the same time, gatekeepers criticize every project for failing to address and ever-expanding checklist of topics that, in a healthier cultural ecosystem, could be addressed by many other available TV shows, films, and exhibitions. The few culturally significant works that rise above the slop are unfairly expected to be everything to everyone. Another reason there aren't more prestigious projects is profit. Ironically, today's cultural institutions from movie studios to art galleries aren't actually geared to create new culture, but to monetize existing IP. As Andrew DeWord notes, they now specialize in derivative media, endlessly recycling proven content across formats, which means the only kind of new culture they're interested in producing is the kind that's preemptively built to be licensed, merchandise, franchised, rebooted, gamified, theme parked, tiktok soundtracks adapted into streaming miniseries bundled into synergy slates and squeezed into cinematic universes. Most new culture isn't made to be a singular achievement, it's made to be interoperable. Any profit that doesn't go directly to shareholders is either used to buy up smaller competitors or to automate content production. The rare prospect of developing new potentially prestigious projects is outsourced to social media, where artists are left to self-fund and compete with each other for virality in an environment that, once again, favors legacy IP. It's not that cultural institutions have abandoned prestige entirely, they've simply tried and failed to scale it. In a previous era, institutions accepted that prestige wasn't for everyone. They funded difficult art on principle, even when it alienated the public, and wore that alienation as a badge of honor. Museums, indie studios, and networks like HBO or ZChannel built their credibility on this self-sacrifice. It was a kind of virtue signaling in the truest sense, it was a moral commitment to difficult often unpopular work, but in late stage and shodification, that model has been replaced. Cultural institutions have learned they can signal virtue without embracing risk. Today, it's more profitable to produce slop geared towards mass appeal than code it in the aesthetics of progress. You ever feel like making someone do what you want, whether they want to or not? It's very European mentality, stemming from the oppressive political and religious structures of the Renaissance, kings and priests with absolute power ruling masses who have none. That was the mentality of the men who discovered America, and it's the mentality our society struggles with today. Instead of funding hard-to-watch films or strange, ambitious exhibitions, institutions now demonstrate their moral standing through maximally inclusive, trauma-focused, socially-affirmational content. In other words, they figured out how to have their virtue and sell it, too. The result is a lot of culture that poses as prestige without ever achieving it. Prestige becomes a mood board of aesthetic references, moral themes and tonal cues recycled from past successes, often matching the production values of serious art without the ambition or difficulty that wants to find it. By trying to manufacture prestige at scale, the culture industry ends up parodying the very thing it's chasing.
